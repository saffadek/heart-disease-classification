The Three Models

How the machine learning works

We trained three different types of classifiers. Each one learns differently. Click a model to see how it works explained simply, what it scored, and why we picked the winner.

Logistic Regression
Simple, fast, interpretable — best params: C = 0.01

How it works — in plain English

Logistic Regression draws a single straight line (or flat surface in many dimensions) that tries to separate sick patients from healthy ones. Every feature gets a weight — like a score. Add up all the weighted scores, and if the total is above a threshold, predict "disease".

Think of it like a points system. Being over 70 adds +15 points. Having diabetes adds +8 points. Exercising regularly subtracts 4 points. If your total hits 20, the model flags you as high risk. Simple, transparent, and explainable to any doctor.

Why we included it

It's the interpretable baseline. Every coefficient is readable. Doctors can understand exactly why a patient was flagged. It also achieved the highest disease recall (80%) — meaning it catches the most sick patients, which matters most in medicine.

What C=0.01 means

C controls how flexible the model is. C=0.01 means strong regularisation — the model stays simple and doesn't chase every quirk in the training data. This prevents overfitting given we only have 177 disease patients to learn from.

How it works — in plain English

A Random Forest builds 100 decision trees, each one trained on a slightly different random slice of the data. Each tree votes on whether a patient has heart disease. The final answer is the majority vote across all 100 trees.

Think of it like asking 100 doctors. Each doctor only sees a random selection of your test results. Some focus on your age and BMI. Others focus on your diabetes and stroke history. You go with whatever diagnosis the majority reaches. No single doctor's bias dominates the result.

Why max_depth=5?

Without a depth limit, trees memorise the training data instead of learning patterns. Limiting each tree to 5 levels keeps them general enough to work on new, unseen patients — the 412 test patients it had never seen before.

Why it's the best model

It has the highest AUC-ROC (0.855) — meaning it is the best at separating sick patients from healthy ones across every possible decision threshold. It also provides feature importance scores, which is how we know age is 28.7% of the prediction.

Support Vector Machine (SVM)
RBF kernel — best params: C=0.1, gamma=0.001

How it works — in plain English

SVM tries to find the widest possible gap (margin) between sick and healthy patients in a high-dimensional space. The RBF kernel lets it bend this boundary into curves, handling non-linear patterns.

Think of it like sorting red and blue marbles. SVM tries to place a dividing wall between them, as far as possible from any marble on either side. The RBF kernel lets that wall be curved, not just flat — so it can separate complicated shapes.

The accuracy trap

SVM scored 84.2% test accuracy — the highest of all three. But this is misleading. With 91.4% of patients healthy, SVM learned to predict "no disease" for borderline cases to maximise accuracy. Its disease recall is only 54.3% — it misses nearly half of sick patients.

Why we still included it

Comparing all three models is required, and SVM teaches us a crucial lesson about why accuracy alone is not enough. The SVM result is the strongest argument for using AUC and recall as primary metrics.

Methodology

How we built it — step by step

Six stages from raw CDC data to a validated model. Every decision is explained.

01📥Sample2,059 at real 8.6% rate from 319,795
02🔍EDAExplore charts, check distributions, zero missing
03🔠EncodeOrdinal ages & health, binary yes/no, one-hot race
04✂Split 80/201,647 train · 412 test (stratified)
05⚖ScaleStandardScaler on train only — no data leakage
06🎯GridSearchF1 scoring · 5-fold · class_weight=balanced
Why GridSearchCV? Instead of guessing the right model settings, we systematically test every combination. For Random Forest alone we tested 3 n_estimators × 4 depths × 3 split sizes = 36 combinations, each evaluated across 5 cross-validation folds. GridSearchCV picked the best automatically.
Why F1 for tuning? F1-score balances catching sick patients (recall) with not crying wolf too often (precision). If we tuned on accuracy, the model would just learn to predict "no disease" for everyone and score 91.4%. F1 forces it to actually learn the minority class.

Responsible AI

What we can't claim — and why that matters

Being honest about the limits of your model is just as important as the results. Here's what we'd need to address before this could ever be used in a real clinic.

⚖
The data isn't everyone
Our CDC data is mostly US respondents and includes race as a feature. The model may predict less accurately for people outside the US, or for racial and income groups underrepresented in the survey. Before deploying in a hospital, you'd need to test on a more diverse dataset (Barocas et al., 2019).
📋
People don't always tell the truth on surveys
The CDC data comes from phone interviews. People under-report smoking, alcohol, and other habits. Some may not know they have undiagnosed conditions. This "noise" is part of why our accuracy is ~82% rather than the 90%+ you'd get from actual blood test data.
🚨
Missing a sick person is worse than a false alarm
If we predict "healthy" for someone who actually has heart disease, they might not get treated in time. That's why we track disease recall separately and tuned on F1-score. In a real deployment, you'd lower the decision threshold below 50% to catch more sick people, even at the cost of more false alarms.
🔒
A tool, not a doctor
This model would support a doctor's decision — never replace it. EU GDPR and US HIPAA both require that any automated health system provides an explanation for its decision and that a qualified human reviews it. The risk calculator on this page is for educational use only.
